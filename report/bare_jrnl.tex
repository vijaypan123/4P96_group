\begin{abstract}
Semi-supervised learning via pseudo-labeling \cite{lee2013pseudolabel} can
substantially reduce the need for labeled data, yet its effectiveness is
sensitive to hyperparameters such as the confidence threshold, pseudo-label
budget, and pseudo-label weight. This paper investigates whether Particle
Swarm Optimization (PSO) \cite{kennedy1995pso} can automatically discover
hyperparameter configurations that outperform carefully designed manual
strategies for a weighted pseudo-labeling pipeline on CIFAR-10. We train a
lightweight CNN under six labeled-data fractions (5\%--50\%), comparing six
expert-crafted manual strategies against a PSO-based search over a
four-dimensional hyperparameter space, each evaluated across five random
seeds. Our results show that well-chosen manual strategies achieve
competitive or marginally higher test accuracy than PSO across all labeling
regimes, with the gap narrowing as labeled data increases. However, manual
tuning requires domain expertise to design effective strategies, whereas PSO
reaches comparable performance without prior knowledge at roughly twice the
computational cost. These findings highlight the trade-off between
human-guided and automated hyperparameter optimization in semi-supervised
settings.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Semi-Supervised Learning and Pseudo-Labeling}

Supervised deep learning achieves strong performance on image classification
benchmarks, but it relies on large quantities of labeled training data.
Obtaining high-quality labels is often expensive and time-consuming, requiring
domain experts or crowd-sourcing pipelines \cite{zhu2005ssl}. Semi-supervised
learning (SSL) addresses this bottleneck by leveraging the abundant supply of
unlabeled data alongside a small labeled set. Among SSL methods,
pseudo-labeling \cite{lee2013pseudolabel} is one of the most widely adopted
due to its simplicity and compatibility with standard training pipelines. The
core idea is to use the model's own high-confidence predictions on unlabeled
samples as surrogate ground-truth labels, iteratively expanding the effective
training set over multiple rounds.

Despite its appeal, pseudo-labeling is notoriously sensitive to its
hyperparameters. The confidence threshold determines how selective the
labeling is; a threshold that is too low admits noisy labels, while one that
is too high yields very few pseudo-labeled samples and offers limited benefit.
The number of pseudo-labels added per round and the weight assigned to them
relative to true labeled samples further compound this sensitivity. In
practice, practitioners manually tune these values through grid search or
intuition, which is both labor-intensive and prone to suboptimal choices.

\subsection{Motivation for Automated Hyperparameter Optimization}

Metaheuristic optimization algorithms offer a principled, automated
alternative to manual search. Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
\cite{kennedy1995pso} is a population-based evolutionary algorithm inspired
by the collective behavior of bird flocking. Each particle in the swarm
represents a candidate hyperparameter configuration and updates its position
based on its own best-known solution and the swarm's global best, converging
toward high-fitness regions of the search space. PSO is well-suited for this
task because it is gradient-free, handles continuous and mixed search spaces
naturally, and requires minimal algorithmic tuning.

\subsection{Contributions}

This work makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We design and implement a weighted pseudo-labeling SSL pipeline on
    CIFAR-10 using a lightweight CNN, where pseudo-labeled samples are down-
    weighted relative to true labels to reduce the impact of labeling noise.
    \item We formulate hyperparameter tuning as a four-dimensional continuous
    optimization problem and implement PSO to search over confidence threshold,
    pseudo-label budget, pseudo-label weight, and learning rate.
    \item We compare PSO-optimized configurations against six manually
    designed strategies across six labeled-data fractions, evaluated with five
    random seeds each, providing a statistically grounded comparison.
    \item We analyze the cost-benefit trade-off between expert-guided and
    automated tuning in the low-data regime.
\end{itemize}


\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Pseudo-Labeling}

Pseudo-labeling was first proposed by Lee \cite{lee2013pseudolabel} as a
straightforward self-training approach where the model assigns labels to
unlabeled samples based on its most confident predictions, then treats those
as real labels in the next round of training. What made it appealing was how
little it changed the standard training setup --- you just add more samples
with predicted labels and keep going. It works well enough when the model
starts from a reasonable place, but falls apart quickly if early predictions
are noisy.

A number of follow-up methods have tried to address that weakness. Xie et
al.\ \cite{xie2020selftraining} showed that adding noise during the student
model's training (while keeping the teacher clean) led to surprisingly strong
results on ImageNet, essentially closing much of the gap to fully supervised
training. Sohn et al.\ \cite{sohn2020fixmatch} went further with FixMatch,
which only accepts a pseudo-label if the model is confident on a weakly
augmented view, and then trains on a strongly augmented version of the same
image. That combination of consistency regularization and aggressive
augmentation pushed performance substantially higher with very few labels.

For our project we deliberately stuck with the simpler iterative formulation
rather than adopting something like FixMatch. The reason is that we wanted the
hyperparameters --- threshold, budget, weight --- to be the main variable
under study, and adding augmentation or consistency losses would have
introduced additional confounders that make it harder to isolate the effect of
those choices.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization}

Picking the right hyperparameters is one of those problems that sounds
straightforward but gets painful fast, especially in SSL where performance can
swing dramatically with small changes to the confidence threshold or
pseudo-label budget. The classical approach is grid search, but that scales
poorly as the number of parameters grows. Bayesian optimization
\cite{snoek2012practical} is a smarter alternative: it fits a surrogate model
(usually a Gaussian process) to the results seen so far and uses that to
decide where to sample next. It tends to find good configurations in fewer
evaluations than grid or random search, but it assumes the objective is fairly
stationary, which is not really the case here since the training set itself
changes between rounds.

Random search \cite{bergstra2012random} is worth mentioning because it
consistently beats grid search in practice when only a few of the parameters
actually matter --- and it is trivially parallelizable. For our setting though,
we suspected all four hyperparameters interact in non-trivial ways, which made
a more directed search strategy like PSO a better fit.

\subsection{PSO for Deep Learning}

PSO has shown up in deep learning mostly for things like neural architecture
search and learning rate scheduling \cite{lorenzo2017psonn}. The main appeal
is that it does not need gradients, so it can optimize anything you can
evaluate, and the swarm structure naturally explores multiple regions of the
search space at once rather than following a single trajectory. The downside is
that each fitness evaluation is a full training run, which gets expensive
quickly.

In our work we apply PSO at the hyperparameter level: each particle encodes a
configuration of threshold, budget, pseudo-label weight, and learning rate,
and its fitness is the best validation accuracy from a short SSL run with that
configuration. This is essentially the same black-box setup used in prior
PSO-based HPO work \cite{lorenzo2017psonn}, just applied to the less-explored
context of semi-supervised learning.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Dataset and Data Splits}

We ran all our experiments on CIFAR-10 \cite{krizhevsky2009cifar10}, which
is a fairly standard choice for SSL research since it is small enough to train
quickly but still non-trivial to classify. The dataset has 60,000 RGB images
at $32{\times}32$ pixels split across 10 classes, with 50,000 for training and
10,000 held out for testing. We normalized each channel using the dataset's
standard mean $(0.4914, 0.4822, 0.4465)$ and standard deviation $(0.2470,
0.2435, 0.2616)$, and deliberately left out any data augmentation. That might
sound like a missed opportunity performance-wise, but we wanted the comparison
between manual and PSO-tuned strategies to hinge purely on the
hyperparameters, not on augmentation effects that would be hard to
disentangle.

For the splits, we first carved out a stratified 10\% of the training set as
a fixed validation set --- this is what both the SSL training loop and the PSO
fitness function use for model selection. The remaining 90\% was then divided
into a labeled pool and an unlabeled pool depending on the labeled fraction
$\rho$, which we varied across $\{5\%, 10\%, 15\%, 20\%, 30\%, 50\%\}$.
Stratified splitting was used throughout so that each class stays
proportionally represented regardless of how small $\rho$ gets. Every split
is seeded, and we run the whole thing over five different seeds to get a
sense of how much results vary just due to randomness in data assignment.

\subsection{Model Architecture}

We kept the model deliberately simple. The goal was not to push
state-of-the-art accuracy on CIFAR-10 but to have something fast enough to
train dozens of times during a PSO run without the whole thing taking days.
The network, which we called \texttt{SimpleCNN}, has two convolutional blocks
followed by a small fully connected head:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Block 1:} Conv2d($3{\to}32$, $3{\times}3$, pad=1) $\to$
    ReLU $\to$ MaxPool($2{\times}2$)
    \item \textbf{Block 2:} Conv2d($32{\to}64$, $3{\times}3$, pad=1) $\to$
    ReLU $\to$ MaxPool($2{\times}2$)
    \item \textbf{Classifier:} Flatten $\to$ Linear($4096{\to}128$) $\to$
    ReLU $\to$ Linear($128{\to}10$)
\end{itemize}

After the two pooling layers the spatial dimensions go from $32{\times}32$
down to $8{\times}8$, giving a flattened feature vector of
$64 \times 8 \times 8 = 4096$ dimensions before the classifier. It is not a
large model by any measure, but for a project focused on the hyperparameter
optimization side of things, it was the right call.

\subsection{Weighted Pseudo-Labeling SSL Pipeline}

The core idea of the pipeline is straightforward: train on whatever labeled
data you have, use the model to label the most confident unlabeled samples,
add those to the training set, and repeat. What we added on top of the basic
recipe is a weighting scheme --- pseudo-labeled samples are not treated the
same as real labeled ones. Instead, they are down-weighted by a factor
$\lambda_p \leq 1.0$ to reduce the damage if some of those pseudo-labels turn
out to be wrong.

Concretely, the per-sample weighted cross-entropy loss at each training step
is:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \cdot
\ell_{\mathrm{CE}}(f(x_i; \theta),\, y_i)
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}

where $w_i = 1.0$ for truly labeled samples and $w_i = \lambda_p$ for
pseudo-labeled ones, $f(\cdot;\theta)$ is the CNN, and $\ell_{\mathrm{CE}}$
is standard cross-entropy. The value of $\lambda_p$ is one of the
hyperparameters we optimize.

After training for $E$ epochs in a given round, we run inference over the
remaining unlabeled pool and collect all samples where the peak softmax
probability exceeds $\tau$. Those are ranked by confidence and the top $K$
get added to the training set for the next round with their predicted label
attached. Algorithm~\ref{alg:ssl} lays this out step by step.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Weighted Pseudo-Labeling SSL}
\label{alg:ssl}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Labeled set $\mathcal{L}$, unlabeled pool $\mathcal{U}$,
         threshold $\tau$, budget $K$, weight $\lambda_p$, rounds $R$,
         epochs $E$, learning rate $\eta$
\STATE Initialize model $f(\cdot;\theta)$
\STATE $\mathcal{P} \leftarrow \emptyset$ \COMMENT{pseudo-label accumulator}
\FOR{$r = 1$ to $R$}
    \STATE Train $f$ on $\mathcal{L} \cup \mathcal{P}$ for $E$ epochs using
           loss (\ref{eq:loss})
    \STATE $\mathcal{C} \leftarrow \{(x, \hat{y}, p) : x \in \mathcal{U},\;
           p = \max_c \sigma_c(f(x)) \geq \tau\}$
    \STATE Sort $\mathcal{C}$ by $p$ descending; take top $K$
    \STATE $\mathcal{P} \leftarrow \mathcal{P} \cup \mathcal{C}$;
           $\mathcal{U} \leftarrow \mathcal{U} \setminus \mathcal{C}$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN Best model by validation accuracy
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

At the end of all rounds, we keep whichever model checkpoint achieved the
highest validation accuracy, not necessarily the last one. Training uses the
Adam optimizer \cite{kingma2014adam}, and we enabled automatic mixed precision
on CUDA to speed things up where hardware allowed.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Search Space}

We identified four hyperparameters that we expected to most influence
pseudo-labeling quality, and those are what both the manual strategies and
PSO operate over:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Confidence threshold} $\tau \in [0.90, 0.99]$
    \item \textbf{Pseudo-label budget per round} $K \in [200, 1500]$
    \item \textbf{Pseudo-label weight} $\lambda_p \in [0.2, 1.0]$
    \item \textbf{Learning rate} $\eta \in [0.0003, 0.002]$
\end{itemize}

The bounds were chosen based on what seemed reasonable for a 10-class problem
with a small model --- for instance, thresholds below 0.90 tend to admit too
much noise, while going above 0.99 at low labeling regimes often yields almost
no pseudo-labels at all. During PSO evaluation we fixed the pipeline at $R = 2$
rounds and $E = 3$ epochs per round, which keeps each fitness evaluation fast
enough to be practical. The manual strategies use the same budget so the
comparison stays fair.

\subsection{Particle Swarm Optimization}

PSO works by maintaining a swarm of $S$ candidate solutions (particles) that
move around the search space guided by their own best-found position and the
best position seen by anyone in the swarm. At each iteration, the velocity and
position of particle $i$ are updated as:

\begin{align}
v_i^{t+1} &= w \cdot v_i^t
            + c_1 r_1 (p_i^{\text{best}} - x_i^t)
            + c_2 r_2 (g^{\text{best}} - x_i^t) \label{eq:vel} \\
x_i^{t+1} &= x_i^t + v_i^{t+1} \label{eq:pos}
\end{align}

Here $w = 0.7$ controls how much momentum carries over from the previous
step, while $c_1 = c_2 = 1.5$ balance the pull toward the particle's own best
versus the global best. The terms $r_1$ and $r_2$ are independent uniform
random draws from $[0, 1]$ that inject some stochasticity into the updates.
After each step, positions are clipped back to the search bounds if they drift
outside.

We used a swarm of $S = 5$ particles over $T = 5$ iterations. Including the
initialization phase, that works out to at most $S(T+1) = 30$ full SSL runs
per labeled fraction. The fitness score for each particle is simply the best
validation accuracy recorded during its corresponding SSL run.

\subsection{Manual Strategies}

To give PSO a meaningful benchmark to beat, we designed six manual
configurations by hand. They are meant to represent a range of intuitions a
practitioner might have: some are conservative (high $\tau$, small $K$,
lower $\lambda_p$) to avoid noisy labels, some are more aggressive (lower
$\tau$, large $K$) to maximize the number of pseudo-labeled samples added per
round, and others sit somewhere in the middle. The threshold spans from
$\tau \leq 0.92$ on the aggressive end up to $\tau \geq 0.97$ for the
conservative ones. Full parameter listings for all six strategies are in the
\texttt{manual\_configs/} folder of our repository.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Every experiment --- both manual and PSO --- is run with five different random
seeds $\{42, 43, 44, 45, 46\}$, giving us enough repetitions to report a
meaningful mean and standard deviation. For each run we evaluate the final
model on the CIFAR-10 test set and record test accuracy and macro-averaged
F1-score. Accuracy is our headline metric, but we included macro F1 as a
sanity check to make sure neither method is quietly ignoring certain classes to inflate its overall number.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why Manual Strategies Remain Competitive}

Honestly, the fact that manual strategies came out ahead was not entirely
surprising to us once we thought about it. When we designed those six
configurations, we were not doing so blindly --- we knew we were working with
CIFAR-10, we knew the model was small, and we had a rough sense from early
experiments of what threshold ranges produced reasonable pseudo-label counts.
PSO does not get any of that for free. It has to figure out what works by
running full SSL evaluations, and with only 30 of those available per labeled
fraction, it simply does not have enough budget to thoroughly explore a
four-dimensional space.

To put it another way: our manual strategies encoded prior knowledge, and PSO
had to earn the same knowledge from scratch. If we had given PSO a swarm of
20 particles over 15 iterations --- around 320 evaluations --- we suspect it
would pull ahead, especially at lower labeled fractions where the landscape is
rougher. But that would have taken far more compute than we had available for
this project, so the 5-particle, 5-iteration setup was a practical compromise.

\subsection{Computational Cost}

The cost difference between the two approaches is significant and worth being
upfront about. Each SSL run with $R = 2$ rounds and $E = 3$ epochs per round
takes roughly as long as training the supervised baseline for 10 epochs. That
means a single PSO run, with its 30 fitness evaluations, costs about
$30\times$ what one baseline training run costs. Evaluating all six manual
strategies only needs 6 runs, so PSO ends up being roughly 5$\times$ more
expensive in terms of total compute.

Whether that cost is worth it really depends on context. In our case, where
we are comparing a known dataset with a fixed architecture, it probably is
not --- the manual strategies already encode enough intuition to do well.
But in a new problem setting where no such intuition exists, spending 5$\times$
more compute to avoid manual trial-and-error could easily be justified. One
direction that would help close this gap is surrogate-assisted PSO, where a
cheap surrogate model predicts fitness before committing to a full SSL run,
or aggressive early stopping to cut off low-performing configurations before
they finish training.

\subsection{Effect of Pseudo-Label Weighting}

One thing we were glad to see confirmed by the results is that down-weighting
pseudo-labeled samples actually matters. We built $\lambda_p < 1.0$ into the
pipeline because it seemed like a sensible guard against noisy labels
propagating too strongly into the model. The PSO runs independently arrived
at the same conclusion --- across all labeled fractions, the swarm converged
to weights well below 1.0, typically in the 0.4--0.6 range, without any
explicit encouragement to do so.

On the manual strategy side, the configurations that used $\lambda_p = 1.0$
(i.e., treating pseudo-labels as equal in importance to real labels) tended to
underperform compared to those that down-weighted them. This is consistent
with what has been shown in the noisy-label learning literature
\cite{natarajan2013noisy}, where over-relying on uncertain supervision tends
to hurt more than it helps once a non-trivial fraction of labels are wrong.

\subsection{Limitations}

We want to be clear about where this work has boundaries. The most obvious one
is the model size --- \texttt{SimpleCNN} is lightweight by design, and it is
possible that a deeper or wider model would interact differently with
pseudo-label noise. Larger models can sometimes absorb noisy supervision
better due to having more capacity, or alternatively suffer more from it due
to memorization effects; we cannot say which without further experiments.

We also skipped data augmentation entirely. That was a deliberate choice to
keep the experimental variables clean, but it means our accuracy numbers are
lower than they would be in a more realistic setup, and it is unclear whether
augmentation would favor one method over the other. Methods like FixMatch
\cite{sohn2020fixmatch} rely heavily on augmentation as part of their core
mechanism, so any comparison to those would need to be done carefully.

Finally, everything here is CIFAR-10. It is a clean, balanced, well-studied
dataset, and results on it do not automatically generalize to noisier datasets,
those with many more classes, or those with significant class imbalance. These
are all reasonable directions for follow-up work.

\section{Conclusion}

This project set out to answer a fairly concrete question: can PSO find
pseudo-labeling hyperparameters that are better than what a person would come
up with manually, and if so, at what cost? Based on our experiments across
six labeled-data fractions and five random seeds, the honest answer is: not
quite, at least not with the compute budget we had. The best manual strategy
edged out PSO at every labeled fraction, with the gap being most noticeable
at 5\% labeled data and shrinking to near-zero by 50\%. 

What that result actually tells us, though, is less about PSO being a bad
algorithm and more about the practical reality of running it on a tight
budget. Thirty fitness evaluations spread across a four-dimensional search
space is not a lot, and the manual strategies had an unfair advantage in the
sense that we designed them with CIFAR-10 and this specific model in mind.
Drop PSO into a new problem where that intuition does not exist, and the
calculus shifts --- spending extra compute to avoid manual trial-and-error
starts to look like a reasonable trade.

The other thing the results confirmed is that pseudo-label weighting is not
just a nice-to-have. PSO independently discovered that $\lambda_p$ well below
1.0 works better, which matches our intuition and the broader noisy-label
literature. That is a small but satisfying validation of the pipeline design.

Going forward, the most impactful change would probably be surrogate-assisted
PSO --- using a cheap model to estimate fitness before committing to a full
SSL run, which could cut the evaluation budget by an order of magnitude.
Adding data augmentation would bring the setup closer to what modern SSL
methods actually use, and comparing against FixMatch \cite{sohn2020fixmatch}
or FlexMatch would give a clearer picture of where simple pseudo-labeling
still holds up and where it falls short.
